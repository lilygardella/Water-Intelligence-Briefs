Market Overview

Arizona's water market is dominated by two large-scale entities — Salt River Project ($4.15B total revenue, primarily electric) and the Central Arizona Project/CAWCD ($309.9M, pure wholesale water) — followed by a long tail of smaller district and irrigation operators. The five entities profiled here represent the primary non-retail water infrastructure operators in the state. Deloitte has limited current engagement in AZ water: SRP has some power advisory relationship; all other entities represent greenfield opportunities.

Key Market Dynamics

  • Colorado River post-2026 operating guideline renegotiation is the defining policy event — affects CAP, SRP customers, YCWUA, Metro Water, and RWCD simultaneously
  • SRP's $6.4B+ ACC-approved bond program is the largest capital program in AZ utility history
  • CAP/CAWCD managing $444M capital reserve build-up ahead of post-2026 infrastructure needs
  • Metro Water's NWRRDS project (mid-2026 completion) expanding CAP water access in Tucson suburbs
  • OT/SCADA cybersecurity is the single most common challenge across all 5 entities
  • RWCD and YCWUA are the lowest-priority targets due to small scale and limited financial transparency

Agency Overview

Salt River Project (SRP) is the umbrella name for two entities: the Salt River Valley Water Users' Association (private water corporation, est. 1903) and the Salt River Project Agricultural Improvement and Power District (electric utility and AZ political subdivision, est. 1937). SRP is the largest utility in Arizona by total revenue ($4.15B FY2024, PwC audited). For water, SRP is a hybrid wholesaler/retailer delivering ~1M acre-feet/year of Salt and Verde River water to agricultural landholders, cities, and municipalities via 7 dams, 6 reservoirs, 131+ miles of canals, and 250+ groundwater wells across a 13,000-square-mile watershed. Water revenue ($25.5M) is a small fraction of total. Both entities governed by elected boards representing landowners through acreage-based proportional voting. ACC approved $6.4B new revenue bonds + $7B refunding bonds in September 2024 — the largest bond authorization in SRP history.

Key Projects & Initiatives
Grid Expansion & 7,000 MW Renewable Build-Out — ISP 2023–2035
Board-approved Integrated System Plan (ISP, October 2023): 7,000 MW new renewables by 2035 (6,000 MW solar, 1,000 MW wind); 1,500 MW battery storage; 1,000 MW pumped hydro; 2,000 MW natural gas peaking. Net-zero carbon by 2050. $6.4B new revenue bonds + $7B refunding bonds approved ACC Sept. 2024. Load growth: Phoenix data centers, semiconductor manufacturing, EV adoption, population growth.
Water Supply Resilience & Underground Storage
Goal: store 1M acre-feet underground by 2035. Manages 13,000-square-mile Salt/Verde watershed. Operates Granite Reef Underground Storage Project (GRUSP) with 9 Valley cities. Provides Groundwater Savings Facility (GSF) services to Phoenix Water, Mesa, Gilbert, Chandler, Scottsdale, Tempe. $6M annual Colorado Water Resiliency Fund.
Advanced Metering Infrastructure & Grid Modernization
Deploying AMI across electric service territory for demand response, TOU pricing, EV load management. Grid monitoring, SCADA modernization, and distribution automation. Ranked #1 reliability for 500K+ customer utilities in the Southwest (2023 SAIDI). Technology modernization to support 7,000 MW renewable integration is a multi-year capital program.
Rate Management & Financial Planning
FPPAM price increase 3.9% effective November 2024. Public pricing process opened December 2024 for base rate adjustments. Revolving credit: Bank of America ($250M, amended Nov. 2025), PNC ($200M), TD Bank ($175M), JP Morgan Chase. Total customer accounts: 1,183,163 as of April 30, 2025 — 2.1% YOY increase.
Key Challenge Areas
Unprecedented Load Growth — Data centers, semiconductor fabs, EV adoption, and population growth driving electricity demand at unprecedented pace. Must accelerate generation and T&D capital investment while managing affordability.
Renewable Integration & Grid Stability — Adding 7,000 MW of intermittent renewables + 1,500 MW storage by 2035 requires sophisticated grid management and demand response. Phoenix's extreme summer peak demand creates central operational tension.
Colorado River Exposure — SRP's primary source is Salt/Verde watershed, but municipal customers rely heavily on CAP/Colorado River water. Ongoing CO River Tier 1 shortage affects SRP's downstream customers and the regional water balance.
Cybersecurity & Critical Infrastructure — Operates critical electric and water infrastructure across 2,900-square-mile territory with extensive SCADA/ICS. Combined water-power infrastructure is a high-value target. OT/ICS security complexity amplified by scale and interconnectedness.
Capital Program Scale & Financing — $6.4B in new revenue bonds for generation and T&D. Managing dozens of simultaneous projects requires sophisticated PMO infrastructure, project controls, and contractor management.
Workforce & Organizational Capacity — Adding 7,000 MW of new resources requires significant workforce scale-up in engineering, construction management, and operations — all in a tight Phoenix metro labor market.

Agency Overview

CAWCD operates the Central Arizona Project (CAP), the 336-mile aqueduct delivering Colorado River water from Lake Havasu to central and southern Arizona. CAP is the largest single source of renewable water in Arizona and the state's only true large-scale pure water wholesaler — delivering up to 1.5 million acre-feet annually of raw, untreated water to municipalities, agricultural districts, and tribes. CAWCD also operates the Central Arizona Groundwater Replenishment District (CAGRD) for 24 active member service areas. Operating revenue $309.9M confirmed from CY2024 ACFR (CliftonLarsonAllen, Dec. 31, 2024) — increase of $23.2M vs. 2023. Governed by a 15-member elected Board (10 Maricopa, 4 Pima, 1 Pinal).

Key Projects & Initiatives
Post-2026 Colorado River Operations — NEPA/DEIS Engagement
Current Lower Basin shortage-sharing guidelines expire end of 2026. CAWCD is Arizona's primary negotiating agency in the DEIS process — the most consequential water policy decision in AZ in decades. Outcome determines CAP delivery reliability for the next 20+ years, directly impacting 80% of AZ population. All 8 KRAs in CAWCD's 2022 Strategic Plan are oriented around managing this uncertainty.
Capital Program & Infrastructure Reliability — 336-Mile Aqueduct
2022 Strategic Plan (8 KRAs, active through 2027) prioritizes infrastructure O&M and technology asset replacement. 336-mile aqueduct — pump stations, canal lining, SCADA, telemetry, control infrastructure built in the 1970s–1990s — requires ongoing rehabilitation. Capital Reserve target raised to $444M (2024 Board decision), up from $281M.
CAGRD — Groundwater Replenishment Expansion & 2025 Plan
CAWCD Board approved the CAGRD 2025 Plan of Operation (Oct. 2024; submitted to ADWR Dec. 2024). Annual replenishment obligation projected to reach 86,900 acre-feet by 2034. CAGRD has accrued 4.47M acre-feet of long-term storage credits. Two new underground storage facilities added since 2015. First CAGRD bond issuance 2019.
Financial Strength & 2026/27 Biennial Budget
2026/2027 Biennial Budget approved by CAWCD Board. Capital reserve raised to $444M. WIFA loan eligibility maintained. Property tax levy in Maricopa, Pima, and Pinal counties (LPV growing 5–6%/year). Investment-grade rated debt. Revenue from water delivery charges, capital charges, CAGRD assessments, and property taxes.
Key Challenge Areas
Post-2026 CO River Shortage & Renegotiation — Ongoing Lower Basin Tier 1 shortage reduces CAP deliveries. Post-2026 NEPA/DEIS process is existential for CAWCD's mission — outcome determines long-term delivery reliability and financial model for decades.
Infrastructure Age & Technology Modernization — CAP aqueduct infrastructure from 1970s–1990s requires sustained rehabilitation. Pump stations, SCADA, canal lining, and OT across 336 miles present a large, complex, multi-decade capital challenge.
CAGRD Replenishment Obligation Growth — Urbanization drives growing CAGRD member service areas and replenishment obligations. Managing water supply procurement, underground storage facility capacity, and member compliance across 24 active areas requires sophisticated data infrastructure.
Cybersecurity & OT Risk — CAWCD operates critical regional infrastructure across 336 miles of aqueduct, pumping plants, and SCADA/telemetry. Water sector is the top ransomware target nationally. OT/ICS security across geographically distributed, aging control systems is a growing priority.

Agency Overview

MDWID (Metro Water District) is the largest domestic water improvement district in Arizona — and the only urban one. Founded in 1992, it provides retail water service to approximately 19,500 connections serving ~50,000 people and hundreds of businesses in northwest, northeast, and southwest metropolitan Tucson across 6 service areas. 95% of customers are single-family residential. Governed by a 5-member elected Board. Fiscal year ends June 30. Financial note: the FY2025 $28.88M figure reflects budgeted operating revenue; exact FY2024 audited actuals are available at metrowater.com/wp-content/uploads/2025-Final-FS.pdf (manual review of p.21 recommended to confirm exact figure). Fitch AA- rated with stable outlook.

Key Projects & Initiatives
NWRRDS — Northwest Recharge, Recovery & Delivery System
Major multi-agency CAP water recovery project in partnership with Towns of Oro Valley and Marana. Construction expected to complete mid-2026. Will provide 115,000+ customers direct access to CAP renewable water supplies in NW Tucson. Most significant capital infrastructure investment in Metro Water's history — funded through WIFA loans and revenue bonds.
5-Year Capital Improvement Program — Aging Infrastructure Replacement
Rolling 5-year CIP prioritizing aging infrastructure replacement: transmission mains, waterlines, meters. Over 100,000 LF of transmission mains and 150,000 LF of aged waterlines already replaced in prior programs. CIP funded through WIFA loans (Series 2005 $15.375M, Series 2007 $12.625M) and revenue bonds (Series 1999 $13M, Series 2002 $10M).
Lead & Copper Rule Compliance & Water Quality
Active service line inventory underway — no lead service lines identified to date. Arsenic treatment and VOC removal systems in place across service areas. Lead & Copper Rule revision preparation included in FY2025/26 budget priorities. Water quality monitoring across 6 service areas with distinct water chemistry.
Water Supply Diversification & CAP Wheeling
CAP wheeling agreement in progress for Metro Southwest service area to provide more reliable base supply. Water supply diversification from groundwater-dependent to CAP renewable base is a long-term strategic priority. Rate sustainability: three-year rate and fee plan in effect; 2023 rate increases implemented. New free online customer portal launched.
Key Challenge Areas
Aging Infrastructure & Capital Funding — Serving ~50K customers across 6 service areas with aging distribution infrastructure. CIP needs are substantial relative to revenue size (~$28–30M); WIFA loans and revenue bonds required to fund capital program. Fitch AA- rating maintains access to capital markets.
CAP Supply Access & Tucson Exchange — Metro Water depends on CAP renewable water via recharge agreements. NWRRDS project (completing mid-2026) will improve direct CAP access for NW Tucson customers. Colorado River shortage conditions create long-term supply uncertainty for CAP-dependent utilities.
Financial Sustainability & Rate Pressure — Operating revenues ~$28–30M require careful rate management to fund capital program without rate shock to residential customers. WIFA AA- rating maintained but leaves less headroom than AA or AA+ rated peers.
Small Scale Procurement Limitations — No dedicated vendor portal; relies on City of Tucson cooperative purchasing and AZ state bid list. Three bids required for purchases >$2,500. Limited procurement infrastructure relative to the scale of capital projects (NWRRDS) underway.

Agency Overview

YCWUA is a private non-profit corporation (501(c)(4)) founded in 1903, one year after the Reclamation Act. Since 1951, YCWUA has operated and maintained the Valley Division of the U.S. Bureau of Reclamation's Yuma Project under federal contract. YCWUA holds the most senior Colorado River water entitlements in Arizona (~360,000 acre-feet/year approved diversion), serves approximately 53,450 irrigable acres (45,000 currently farmed), provides over 95% of the City of Yuma's municipal water supply (~18,000 acre-feet/year), and serves the Cocopah Indian Tribe (~6,000 acre-feet/year). YCWUA also operates the Siphon Drop hydroelectric power plant under federal contract. As a 501(c)(4) non-profit, financial disclosure is via IRS Form 990 — not an ACFR. FY2024 total revenues ~$13.0M; total assets $55.9M (Form 990, EIN 86-0069710, via CauseIQ/ProPublica). 68 employees. Official website (ycwua.org) is currently under redesign with minimal content.

Agency Overview

Roosevelt Water Conservation District (RWCD) is a municipal corporation and political subdivision of the State of Arizona, established in 1917. It is an irrigation district serving approximately 40,000 acres in southeast Maricopa County, with its service area overlapping the rapidly urbanizing cities of Chandler, Gilbert, and Mesa. RWCD holds a 5.6% entitlement to annual surface water delivered from SRP's Salt and Verde River system and is transitioning from predominantly agricultural to urban customers — shifting from ~500 agricultural users to over 2,000 urban customers in under 7 years. Financial data note: RWCD's official website (rwcd.net) has minimal publicly available financial documents. Revenue estimate of ~$5.6M is from third-party sources (ZoomInfo/Kona Equity) only. A public records request to RWCD or Maricopa County assessor review is required to confirm. RWCD is currently involved in litigation with the City of Chandler over the 2002 domestic water agreement. Headquartered at 2344 S. Higley Rd, Gilbert, AZ 85295.

RWCD has transitioned from ~500 agricultural users to over 2,000+ urban customers in under 7 years.
