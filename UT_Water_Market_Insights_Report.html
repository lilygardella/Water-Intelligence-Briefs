Utah Water Market Context

Utah's water sector is undergoing a period of significant investment and strategic transformation driven by three converging forces: explosive population growth along the Wasatch Front (Utah is one of the fastest-growing states in the U.S.), the Great Salt Lake crisis (the lake has lost two-thirds of its historic volume, driving statewide conservation mandates), and significant federal funding availability through IIJA/BIL water programs. The five entities in this brief — CUWCD, JVWCD, WCWCD, WBWCD, and MWDSLS — are the largest water conservancy districts in the state, collectively serving approximately 3+ million Utahns across the Wasatch Front, St. George metro, and surrounding counties.

All five entities face common challenges: aging infrastructure requiring rehabilitation, Great Salt Lake conservation mandates, and capital programs funded through a mix of rate revenue, property taxes, bonds, and federal grants. No confirmed Deloitte relationships exist at any of the five Utah entities — all are new pursuit territory.

Entity Overview

The Central Utah Water Conservancy District (CUWCD) is the largest water conservancy district in Utah, serving approximately 2 million people across 7 counties (Salt Lake, Uintah, Duchesne, Juab, Wasatch, Summit, Utah). CUWCD is tasked with repaying the federal Central Utah Project (CUP) — managed under the Central Utah Project Completion Act (CUPCA) — and delivering clean water through a vast network of dams, hydropower plants, pipelines, and treatment facilities. CUWCD directly delivers water to 20 entities (14 municipalities + 6 improvement/conservation districts) with an additional 22 indirect customers. FY2025 operating revenue of $110.3M confirmed from audited financial statements (cuwcd.gov). Net position: $1,292.5M at June 30, 2025. Total assets: $2.1B. FY2024-25 budget: $382M. Capital Projects Fund balance: $303.8M at June 30, 2025 — the largest single capital investment signal in the Utah water portfolio. Fitch AA+ rated.
