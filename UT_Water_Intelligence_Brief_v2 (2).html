Market Overview

Utah's water district market is anchored by CUWCD ($110.3M operating revenue, $2.1B total assets) as the largest conservancy district in the state, followed by JVWCD ($75.7M) serving Salt Lake County's fastest-growing cities. WBWCD operates the largest secondary irrigation system in the western U.S. and serves 5 counties. MWDSLS is a key wholesale provider for Salt Lake City and Sandy City. WCWCD serves the fastest-growing county in the nation — St. George and Washington County. All five entities face the same core challenges: population growth, aging infrastructure, Great Salt Lake health, and long-term water supply development.

Key Market Dynamics

  • Utah is one of the fastest-growing states in the U.S. — all 5 entities face sustained demand growth
  • Great Salt Lake decline is an explicit strategic priority for JVWCD, CUWCD, and WBWCD
  • Wasatch Fault seismic risk creates infrastructure resilience urgency across Salt Lake Valley entities
  • JVWCD's Digital Twin pilot (Sept. 2025) is the most current confirmed technology investment signal

Agency Overview

CUWCD is the largest water district in Utah, delivering clean water to customers across seven counties — Salt Lake, Uintah, Duchesne, Juab, Wasatch, Summit, and Utah. Established in 1964, CUWCD serves as the local repayment agency and operator of the Central Utah Project (CUP), a vast federal water infrastructure system. FY2025 operating revenue $110.3M; total revenue $267.3M; total assets $2.1B; net position $1,292.5M (2025 Audited Financial Statements, cuwcd.gov). Budget $398M (FY2025–26). Directly delivers water to 20 entities (14 municipalities + 6 improvement/conservation districts). Fitch AA+ rated. Operates 3 water treatment facilities, 2 hydroelectric plants, 9 reservoirs.

Key Projects & Initiatives
Central Water Project (CWP) — $303.8M Capital Projects Fund
CWP Fund balance $303.8M at June 30, 2025. Primary vehicle for new water infrastructure — conveyance, storage, and treatment capacity expansion. The Olmsted replacement work (aging infrastructure) is a major active component. Total governmental fund balance: $453.5M (General $45.6M, CUPCA $18.8M, CWP $39.5M, Debt Service $45.8M, Capital Projects $303.8M).
Conservation & Demand Management — 25% Reduction Target by 2050
2025 Water Conservation Plan (adopted 2025) targets 25% per-capita water use reduction by 2050. Conservation positioned as necessary but insufficient — new supply development also required. Regional conservation collaboration with cities/agencies; aggressive non-functional turf reduction across 7 counties.
Jordanelle Hydroelectric & Renewable Energy
CUWCD operates the Jordanelle Hydroelectric Plant as a separate enterprise fund — generating power revenue diversification from water deliveries. June Sucker Recovery Agreement Fund is a private purpose trust fund reflecting CUWCD's federal environmental obligations under CUPCA. Hydroelectric operations support financial sustainability and environmental stewardship mandate.
New Water Supply Development & Regional Collaboration
CUWCD positions itself as a regional convener across its 20+ customer agencies. New supply sources actively being developed — conservation alone insufficient per district's own strategic framework. Regional collaboration with municipalities, USBR, and ADWR is a declared strategic pillar. Great Salt Lake health and resilience is an explicitly stated environmental priority.

Water Supply Development — Long-Term Demand Growth — Utah County faces sustained population growth. CUWCD explicitly acknowledges conservation alone is insufficient. New supply development requires decades of planning and capital investment.
Aging Infrastructure & Olmsted Replacement — CUP infrastructure requires replacement and rehabilitation. Olmsted replacement is an active capital priority. Managing dispersed dams, reservoirs, treatment plants, and pipelines across 7 counties creates sustained O&M complexity.
Federal CUPCA Partnership & USBR Relationship — Operates under CUPCA (1992) with DOI CUPCA program manager. Federal budget pressures and CUPCA cost-sharing requirements create multi-year capital program uncertainty.
Great Salt Lake Resilience & Environmental Obligations — GSL health is an explicit strategic priority. June Sucker Recovery Agreement Fund reflects federal compliance obligations. Climate change and drought increase complexity for 7-county water supply planning.
Financial Sustainability — Debt Service & Capital Reserve — Managing $398M annual budget, five distinct government funds, Jordanelle enterprise fund, and CUPCA cost-sharing requires sophisticated financial planning as capital program scale grows.
Cybersecurity & Operational Technology — Operates critical infrastructure including dams, hydropower plants, treatment facilities, and pipelines across 7 counties. SCADA and ICS across distributed infrastructure create OT cybersecurity exposure consistent with national water sector risk trends.

Agency Overview

WCWCD is a not-for-profit public agency established in 1962 to manage Washington County's water needs in southern Utah — one of the fastest-growing regions in the U.S. Provides wholesale water to Washington City and 8 municipalities under contractual agreements; also operates 2 retail water systems. 2024 Audited Financial Statements (Hinton Burdick, FY ended Dec. 31, 2024): operating revenue $26.1M; total revenue $111.6M; total assets $722.7M; net position $644M. Note: $26.1M reflects water rate/operational income; $111.6M total includes capital contributions, impact fees, grants, and property taxes. AA+ (Fitch) / AA (S&P). Manages $1B+ in infrastructure capable of producing 60M+ gallons per day.

Key Projects & Initiatives
Lake Powell Pipeline Project — Regional Supply Planning
WCWCD is the lead agency for the proposed Lake Powell Pipeline (lpputah.org) — a major water supply augmentation project that would bring Colorado River water from Lake Powell to Washington County. The project represents the most significant long-term water supply development effort in southern Utah. Current sources are finite; pipeline development requires decades.
Active Capital Projects — Quail Creek WTP & Pump Stations
Quail to Cottam Pump Stations Project bids opened November 26, 2024 (Bowen Collins & Associates as engineer, confirmed from wcwcd.gov). Hydrologic Services SOQ active November 2024. Three governmental fund structure: General Fund, Special Revenue Fund (Virgin River Program), Capital Projects Fund — combined $255M balance.
20-Year Water Plan — 11,400 AF Conservation Target by 2042
20-Year Plan (adopted 2024) projects conservation will reduce water demand of pre-2023 connections by 11,400 acre-feet between 2022 and 2042. Four pillars: regulation, pricing, incentives, and education. Regional Reuse System (recycled water for non-potable uses) is an active infrastructure program. Sand Hollow Reservoir provides 300,000+ acre-feet underground storage capacity.
Regional Reuse System & Secondary Water
WCWCD operates a Regional Reuse System delivering recycled water for non-potable uses — key demand management in an arid, rapidly growing region. Secondary water systems support agricultural and landscape irrigation. Hydropower plants at dam facilities provide energy revenue diversification.
Key Challenge Areas
Rapid Population Growth — St. George and Washington County consistently rank among the top fastest-growing counties nationally. WCWCD must plan, finance, and build water supply and infrastructure faster than almost any other Intermountain West water district.
Colorado River Exposure & Lake Powell Pipeline — Washington County relies on Virgin River and underground storage as primary current sources. Long-term demand growth requires the Lake Powell Pipeline — but NEPA, CO River shortage conditions, and interstate compact complexities create significant timeline risk.
Capital Funding Complexity — Three revenue streams: water rates (operations), limited property taxes (watershed/environmental), and impact fees (new infrastructure). Managing these streams across a rapidly growing service area requires sophisticated rate design, impact fee studies, and financial planning.
Regulatory Complexity — Water Rights & Interstate — Southern Utah water development requires navigating water rights, Virgin River Compact obligations, Lake Powell Pipeline NEPA/EIS permitting, and coordination with Nevada and Arizona under the Virgin River Basin Compact.

Agency Overview

Weber Basin Water Conservancy District (WBWCD) is a wholesale and retail water provider serving 5 counties in northern Utah: Box Elder, Davis, Morgan, Summit, and Weber. WBWCD supplies 5 categories of water: Wholesale & Retail Secondary Irrigation, Wholesale & Retail Agriculture Irrigation, Groundwater Replacement, Treated Municipal & Industrial, and Untreated Municipal & Industrial. Notably, WBWCD operates the largest secondary irrigation system in the western United States. Financial figures from PPT (citing official WBWCD budget documents): FY2023 total annual revenue $73.76M; FY2025 budgeted sales $47.3M; FY2025 total budgeted revenue $108M; FY2024 net position $380.5M; 2024 total assets & deferred outflows $599.4M. Agency Overview

Metropolitan Water District of Salt Lake & Sandy (MWDSLS) is a wholesale treated water supplier serving Salt Lake City and Sandy City in the Salt Lake Valley. It supplies roughly 50–75% of Sandy City's water supply and is a key Wasatch Front conveyance and treatment operator. MWDSLS serves over 400,000 people. Financial figures from PPT (citing 2024 and 2023 financial statements): 2024 operating revenue $24.6M; 2024 net position $246M; 2024 total assets $574M; 2024 total assets and deferred outflows $590M; FY2026 budgeted revenue $111.4M; FY2025 capital budget $47.5M; FY2026 projected capital $35.7–$37M. FY2026 capital funding sourced from rate revenue, bond proceeds, federal grants, and federal loans — a strong signal of complex financing advisory needs. MWDSLS appears to operate under the Utah Procurement Code.

$47.5M FY2025 Capital Budget — Major Projects Active
MWDSLS has a $47.5M FY2025 capital budget — significant relative to $24.6M operating revenue, signaling a capital-intensive growth and infrastructure phase. FY2026 capital projected at $35.7–$37M. Capital funding from rate revenue, bond proceeds, federal grants, and federal loans.
