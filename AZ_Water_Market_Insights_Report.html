Arizona Water Market Context

Arizona's water sector is defined by two existential challenges: the post-2026 Colorado River operating guideline renegotiation and the relentless growth pressure of the Phoenix and Tucson metro areas. The Colorado River delivers approximately 40% of Arizona's water supply via the Central Arizona Project (CAP) — the 336-mile aqueduct operated by CAWCD. The post-2026 Lower Basin shortage guideline renegotiation is the most consequential water policy decision in Arizona in decades, and its outcome will govern supply certainty, rate trajectories, and capital program priorities for all five entities in this brief. Simultaneously, SRP is executing the largest capital program in Arizona utility history — $6.4B in ACC-approved revenue bonds for 7,000 MW of renewables, 1,000 MW pumped hydro, and grid modernization — driven by Phoenix metro data center, semiconductor, and EV load growth at an unprecedented pace.

The five entities covered in this brief represent Arizona's most significant water infrastructure organizations. No confirmed Deloitte footprint has been confirmed at CAP/CAWCD, Metro Water, YCWUA, or RWCD. SRP has a limited prior power advisory relationship. RWCD is flagged as a monitor-only entity pending resolution of the Chandler v. RWCD Arizona Supreme Court case.

Entity Overview

Salt River Project (SRP) is the umbrella name for two separate but closely linked entities: the Salt River Valley Water Users' Association (a private water corporation, established 1903) and the Salt River Project Agricultural Improvement and Power District (an electric utility and political subdivision of Arizona, established 1937). SRP serves over 2 million people in the Phoenix metropolitan area, delivering approximately 1 million acre-feet per year of Salt River and Verde River surface water via 7 dams, 6 reservoirs, 131+ miles of canals, and 250+ groundwater wells. SRP's water revenue ($25.5M, FY2024) is a small fraction of its total $4.15B operating revenues — the primary advisory opportunity is the electric capital program and combined water-power OT environment. The Arizona Corporation Commission approved $6.4B in new revenue bonds plus $7B in refunding bonds in September 2024 — the largest single bond authorization in SRP history. Total customer accounts reached 1,183,163 as of April 30, 2025 (2.1% year-over-year growth).

Pressing Business Issues
Issue 1
$6.4B Capital Program Delivery — 7,000 MW Renewables by 2035
CRITICAL
SRP's board-approved Integrated System Plan (ISP, October 2023) calls for 7,000 MW new renewables by 2035 (6,000 MW solar, 1,000 MW wind), 1,500 MW battery storage, 1,000 MW pumped hydro, and 2,000 MW natural gas peaking. Net-zero carbon by 2050. ACC approved $6.4B new revenue bonds (Sept. 2024). Managing dozens of simultaneous capital projects across generation, T&D, and substations — while maintaining the #1 Southwest reliability ranking (2023 SAIDI, per EIA) — creates acute PMO, project controls, and contractor management demand.
Source: SRP ISP (October 2023); ACC approval September 2024; SRP FY2025 Combined Financial Statements (PwC audited).
Issue 2
Unprecedented Load Growth — Phoenix Metro Data Centers, EV, Semiconductors
CRITICAL
Phoenix metro data center construction, semiconductor manufacturing (TSMC, Intel, Samsung), EV adoption, and continued population growth are driving electricity demand at a pace not seen in decades. SRP must accelerate generation and T&D capital investment while managing affordability. Load growth is the direct driver behind the $6.4B bond authorization.
Source: SRP ISP (October 2023); SRP FY2025 Combined Financial Statements.
Issue 3
Cybersecurity & OT/ICS Risk — Combined Water-Power Infrastructure
HIGH
SRP operates critical electric and water infrastructure across a 2,900-square-mile service territory with extensive SCADA/ICS systems — electric grid ICS, water delivery SCADA, pumping plant controls, and AMI. The expanding capital program introduces new technologies and third-party contractors into the OT environment. Combined water-power utility infrastructure is a high-value target for nation-state and ransomware actors. The water sector is the #1 ransomware target in U.S. critical infrastructure.
Source: SRP infrastructure profile; EPA water sector OT guidance; national critical infrastructure reporting.
Issue 4
Workforce & Organizational Capacity — Capital Program Scale-Up
HIGH
Adding 7,000 MW of new resources while maintaining existing infrastructure requires significant workforce expansion in engineering, construction management, and operations — in a highly competitive Phoenix metro labor market where SRP competes with TSMC, Intel, Google, and other major employers for the same talent pool.
Source: SRP ISP; public labor market reporting, Phoenix metro 2024–2025.
Issue 5
Water Supply & Colorado River Exposure
ELEVATED
While SRP's primary water source is the Salt/Verde watershed, its municipal customers also rely heavily on CAP/Colorado River water. SRP also provides Groundwater Savings Facility (GSF) services using CAP water. Ongoing CO River Tier 1 shortage conditions affect the regional water balance underpinning Phoenix metro growth.
Source: SRP water operations documentation; USBR shortage determination.

Entity Overview

The Central Arizona Water Conservation District (CAWCD) operates the Central Arizona Project (CAP) — the 336-mile aqueduct delivering Colorado River water from Lake Havasu to central and southern Arizona. CAP is the largest single source of renewable water in Arizona and the state's only pure large-scale water wholesaler, delivering up to 1.5 million acre-feet annually to municipalities, agricultural districts, and Native American communities. CAWCD also operates the Central Arizona Groundwater Replenishment District (CAGRD), managing groundwater replenishment obligations for 24 active member service areas. Governed by a 15-member elected Board of Directors (10 Maricopa, 4 Pima, 1 Pinal county). CY2024 ACFR (CLA audited, December 31, 2024) confirmed operating revenues of $309.9M — an increase of $23.2M over 2023. GFOA Certificate of Achievement for Excellence in Financial Reporting, 13th consecutive year.

Pressing Business Issues
Issue 1
Post-2026 Colorado River Operating Guidelines — Existential for CAWCD
CRITICAL
Current Lower Basin shortage-sharing guidelines expire end of 2026. CAWCD is Arizona's primary negotiating agency in the Bureau of Reclamation's DEIS process. Current Tier 1 shortage reduces Arizona's Colorado River entitlement by 512,000 acre-feet. The outcome will govern CAP delivery certainty through approximately 2046 and is the defining strategic challenge for CAWCD — all 8 Key Result Areas in the 2022 Strategic Plan are oriented around managing this uncertainty.
Source: USBR DEIS process; CAWCD 2022 Strategic Plan (confirmed cawcd.gov); CY2024 ACFR.
Issue 2
CAP Aqueduct Infrastructure Age & Technology Modernization
HIGH
CAP aqueduct infrastructure from the 1970s–1990s requires sustained rehabilitation. Pump stations, SCADA systems, canal lining, and operational technology across 336 miles present a large, complex, multi-decade capital program challenge. Capital Reserve target raised to $444M (2024 Board decision, up from $281M) to fund upcoming large capital projects.
Source: CAWCD 2022 Strategic Plan; CY2024 ACFR (capital reserve confirmed).
Issue 3
CAGRD Replenishment Obligation Growth — 100-Year Planning Horizon
HIGH
CAWCD Board unanimously approved the CAGRD 2025 Plan of Operation (October 2024; submitted to ADWR December 2024). Annual replenishment obligation projected to reach 86,900 acre-feet by 2034 for current and projected members. CAGRD has accrued 4.47M acre-feet of long-term storage credits since inception. Two new underground storage facilities added since 2015 (SEAP and Olberg Dam). First CAGRD bond issuance executed in 2019; bonding authority established for future capital needs.
Source: CAGRD 2025 Plan of Operation (cagrd.com); CAWCD Board October 2024.
Issue 4
Cybersecurity & OT/ICS Risk — Distributed Aqueduct Infrastructure
HIGH
CAWCD operates SCADA and industrial control systems across 336 miles of aqueduct, 14 pumping plants, and distributed telemetry infrastructure — much of it aging and originally designed without cybersecurity considerations. The water sector is the #1 ransomware target nationally. EPA guidance increasingly requires formal OT risk assessments from large water systems.
Source: CAWCD infrastructure profile; EPA water sector OT guidance.
Issue 5
Federal Funding & USBR Partnership Uncertainty
HIGH
CAWCD operates federally owned infrastructure under contract with USBR. Federal budget pressures and shifting Reclamation priorities create uncertainty around O&M cost-sharing, capital investment, and the post-2026 shortage framework. WIFA received $1B in new appropriations (2024) for long-term water supply projects. WIFA board authorized staff to begin discussions with IDE Technologies on a potential Sea of Cortez desalination plant in Puerto Peñasco, Mexico — CAWCD would be the likely delivery and integration partner.
Source: WIFA 2024 appropriations; CAP public communications.

Entity Overview

Metro Water District (formally the Metropolitan Domestic Water Improvement District, or MDWID) is a retail water utility serving northern Maricopa County and the northwest Phoenix metropolitan area — one of the fastest-growing areas in the United States. Metro Water receives treated water via the Northwest Regional Domestic Water System (NWRRDS), a CAP-fed pipeline, and distributes it to approximately 500,000 residents across communities including Peoria, northwest Phoenix, Anthem, and surrounding areas. The FY2026 adopted budget is $36.55M total with $4.13M depreciation/amortization, and 81.92% of operating revenue from metered water sales. A rate hearing was held on May 15, 2023 approving rate changes for three fiscal years including FY2026 with no rate changes in FY2026. Source: metrowater.com/about-us/budget-audit/ (confirmed, accessed May 2026).

CAP Supply Dependency & Post-2026 Colorado River Risk
CRITICAL
Metro Water's primary supply is the NWRRDS pipeline, which is entirely CAP-fed. The post-2026 Colorado River operating guideline renegotiation directly threatens Metro Water's supply reliability. Any reduction in CAP deliveries under Tier 1, Tier 2, or structural shortage scenarios directly impacts Metro Water's ability to serve the fastest-growing area in Maricopa County.
Source: metrowater.com; USBR DEIS process.

Entity Overview

The Yuma County Water Users' Association (YCWUA) is an agricultural water delivery organization that holds the most senior Colorado River water rights in Arizona — a priority date of 1903 — and delivers water to approximately 65,000 acres of farmland in Yuma County, Arizona's most productive agricultural valley. YCWUA operates under contract with USBR as a federally chartered reclamation entity. Its senior water rights give it priority over junior rights holders (including CAP) under Colorado River shortage conditions — making YCWUA relatively insulated from Colorado River shortage impacts. Revenue of ~$13M confirmed from IRS Form 990 via ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer (FY2024). Entity type: nonprofit/irrigation association — not a government entity.

Pressing Business Issues
Issue 1
Colorado River Policy & Post-2026 Framework
ELEVATED
While YCWUA's senior water rights (1903 priority date) provide relative insulation from shortage impacts vs. junior CAP rights, the post-2026 operating guideline renegotiation creates policy uncertainty. Changes to the Lower Basin operational framework, endangered species requirements, and shortage-sharing mechanisms all have potential implications for YCWUA's long-term operations.
Source: USBR DEIS process; YCWUA federal charter.
Issue 2
Federal Funding — BIL/IRA Water Programs
ELEVATED
YCWUA is eligible for USBR Water Smart and BIL/IIJA irrigation efficiency, water recycling, and infrastructure grants. At ~$13M in annual revenue, federal grants are disproportionately important for capital investment. YCWUA's federal reclamation status also makes it eligible for USBR WaterSMART program funding.
Source: USBR Water Smart program; BIL water provisions.

  • Water Rights: Most senior Colorado River water rights in Arizona (1903 priority date). Not subject to CAP Tier 1 shortage cuts — insulated relative to junior rights holders.
