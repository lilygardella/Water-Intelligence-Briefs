Who They Are

The Metropolitan Water District of Southern California (Metropolitan, or "Met") is the largest treated water wholesaler in the United States and a state-chartered public agency created by California state law in 1928. Metropolitan's mission is to provide its 26 member agencies — a voluntary cooperative of 14 cities, 11 municipal water districts, and one county water authority — with adequate and reliable supplies of high-quality water for the nearly 19 million people of Southern California. The six-county service area spans Los Angeles, Orange, San Diego, Riverside, San Bernardino, and Ventura counties — roughly half of California's economy. Metropolitan imports water from two sources: the Colorado River via the 242-mile Colorado River Aqueduct (CRA), and Northern California via the Edmund G. Brown California Aqueduct (State Water Project, or SWP). It operates five water treatment plants (among the largest in the nation), 20 reservoirs, 24 dams, and 830 miles of pipelines. Metropolitan maintains an annual operating budget of nearly $2 billion and a $4.8 billion biennial budget (FY2024/25–FY2025/26). It is a Aa1 Moody's / AA rated entity. In October 2025, Adán Deshmukh was named General Manager, replacing Adel Hagekhalil.

Why This Matters to Deloitte — Right Now

New General Manager — October 2025

Adán Deshmukh was named General Manager in October 2025. A new GM is one of the most reliable signals of organizational openness to new advisory relationships. Deshmukh will be establishing his leadership agenda, assembling priorities, and making decisions about external partners over the next 12–18 months. This is the single most important relationship-building window at Met in years.

Business Model Transformation — CAMP4W

Metropolitan's Climate Adaptation Master Plan for Water (CAMP4W) — a board-approved decision-making framework adopted April 2025 — is fundamentally reorienting how Metropolitan evaluates and selects capital investments. CAMP4W is simultaneously a planning exercise, a business model transformation, and a stakeholder engagement challenge. Deloitte's strategy and analytics practices are directly applicable. The framework is brand-new and external advisory has not yet been competitively established around it.

SDCWA Litigation Settlement — June 2025

Metropolitan and the San Diego County Water Authority concluded 15 years of litigation on June 2, 2025. The settlement "introduces the most significant change to Metropolitan's business model" (Board Chair Ortega, 2025) — allowing member agencies to exchange surplus conserved supplies for sale to other agencies for the first time. This structural business model change creates immediate demand for financial modeling, rate design, and member agency relations advisory work.

Pure Water Southern California — $205M+ Federal Commitment

Pure Water Southern California (PWSC) has secured $125.5M in FY2024/25 USBR/IRA grants, $26.2M in additional federal grants (November 2024), and $80M from the State of California. The Draft EIR was released May 14, 2025. If approved at full scale, PWSC will be one of the largest water purification programs in the world — producing 150 MGD for 1.5 million people. This program is generating advisory, PMO, and program management procurement demand now.

$10.3M Enterprise Content Management Phase II

Metropolitan's FY2025/26 budget explicitly allocates $10.3M for Phase II of its Enterprise Content Management (ECM) system deployment, $6.68M for IT Applications, and $3.3M for Enterprise Data Analytics — all confirmed from the adopted Biennial Budget (p.315 and p.247). Metropolitan uses Oracle e-Business Suite for financials, procurement, project management, and grants management. These are live technology procurements that Deloitte's Digital Engineering practice can directly support.

Post-2026 Colorado River — Existential Supply Question

Metropolitan's current Lower Basin water rights expire under the existing operating guidelines in 2026. The Bureau of Reclamation's DEIS process for post-2026 guidelines is underway. As the largest Colorado River user in Southern California, Metropolitan is a lead stakeholder in these negotiations — alongside CAP/CAWCD, SNWA, and LADWP. Scenario planning, financial modeling under shortage conditions, and stakeholder engagement strategy are all live advisory needs.

The Scale Context

Metropolitan is not a target comparable to the AZ and UT water districts in this research portfolio. It is an order of magnitude larger — $2B+ in annual operating revenue, a $4.8B biennial budget, 1,631 employees, 19 million customers, and infrastructure that spans six counties. For comparison: the entire AZ top-5 portfolio (SRP + CAP + Phoenix Water + Tucson Water + Metro Water) totals roughly $5B in combined revenue — Metropolitan alone is $2B of that. This means two things for Deloitte: (1) the engagements are correspondingly larger and more complex, and (2) the competition is correspondingly more intense — KPMG, Accenture, IBM, Black & Veatch, Carollo Engineers, and other large firms are all present or pursuing Metropolitan. Deloitte's differentiation must be in strategy, analytics, digital, and OT security — lanes that engineering firms cannot easily occupy.

Strategic Context — Priorities, Challenges & Operating Environment
Five Strategic Priorities (FY2024/25–FY2025/26 Biennial Budget, p.55)
SP #1 — Empower the Workforce & Promote Diversity, Equity and Inclusion
Workforce development, succession planning, and DEI are named strategic priorities in the Biennial Budget. Metropolitan explicitly notes that "leading the way" in water supply requires a workforce "as agile and efficient as the water delivery systems we develop and operate." Succession planning, diversity, and asset retention are cited as vital organizational focus areas. With ~1,631 employees and a massive capital program ahead, workforce planning is a live need.
SP #2 — Sustain Metropolitan's Mission with a Strengthened Business Model
The SDCWA litigation settlement (June 2025) and CAMP4W framework together represent Metropolitan's most significant business model transformation in decades. For the first time, the settlement allows member agencies to exchange and sell surplus conserved supplies. CAMP4W creates a new standardized methodology for evaluating capital investments across six criteria: reliability, affordability, environmental co-benefits, equity, financial sustainability, and community impact. Business model advisory, financial modeling, and change management are directly applicable.
SP #3 — Adapt to Changing Climate and Water Resources
CAMP4W (Climate Adaptation Master Plan for Water) was board-approved in April 2025. Metropolitan is now evaluating proposed capital projects — including Pure Water Southern California, Sites Reservoir, Delta Conveyance Project, and increased conservation investments — against CAMP4W criteria. The 2025 Urban Water Management Plan (UWMP) and Water Shortage Contingency Plan (WSCP) are under active development (public hearing April 13, 2026). Seismic resilience is a named sub-priority with its own annual report series (2025 Seismic Resilience Report published August 2025).
SP #4 — Protect Public Health, the Regional Economy & Metropolitan's Assets
This priority drives the largest share of technology investment. The Biennial Budget explicitly calls for: applying innovation and technology across project lifecycles (design, construction, operations, maintenance, replacement); implementing the Enterprise Content Management system ($10.3M Phase II); developing procurement policies that prioritize sustainable products and practices; and managing cybersecurity and OT risk. Oracle e-Business Suite is the enterprise platform for financials, procurement, project management, and grants management.
SP #5 — Partner with Interested Parties and the Communities We Serve
Metropolitan serves 26 member agencies, each represented on the 38-member Board of Directors. Managing the member agency relationship — rate setting, supply allocation, service agreements, communications — is a permanent strategic challenge. The SDCWA litigation settlement and post-2026 Colorado River negotiations both create acute near-term member agency communications and stakeholder engagement needs. Metropolitan's Board includes business, professional, and civic leaders from across Southern California.
CAMP4W — The Overarching Strategic Framework
The Climate Adaptation Master Plan for Water (CAMP4W) is Metropolitan's new integrated decision-making framework for climate-driven capital investment. Board-approved April 2025. Evaluates proposed projects across 6 criteria: reliability, affordability, environmental co-benefits, equity, financial sustainability, community impact. Projects under assessment: Pure Water Southern California, Sites Reservoir, Delta Conveyance, conservation programs, High Desert Water Bank. Project assessments will be presented to the Board through 2025–2026 — the decision-making window is now open.
2026 Annual Operating Plan — Core Priorities

The 2026 Annual Operating Plan (published February 2026) establishes Metropolitan's operational framework for the year. Its stated core operating principles are: (1) meet member agency demands; (2) meet water quality requirements; (3) manage storage under water surplus and drought management principles; (4) manage maintenance and shutdowns; (5) meet blending objectives; and (6) maximize hydroelectric power production. The plan's core uncertainty driver is State Water Project allocation levels — below-30% allocation implies storage withdrawals; above-30% implies storage gains. The bottom-line objective: deliver reliable, compliant water service while preserving drought resilience through disciplined storage, flexible operations, coordinated maintenance, and adaptive use of SWP and Colorado River supplies.

Key Challenge Areas
Post-2026 Colorado River Operating Guidelines — Current Lower Basin shortage-sharing guidelines expire end of 2026. Metropolitan is Arizona's counterpart in these negotiations — the largest Colorado River user in Southern California. The post-2026 framework will determine Metropolitan's supply certainty, rate trajectory, and capital program for the next 20+ years. CAMP4W was designed specifically to manage this uncertainty.
Pure Water Southern California Program Delivery — PWSC is potentially one of the world's largest water purification programs: 150 MGD for 1.5 million people. Draft EIR released May 14, 2025. $205M+ in federal/state grants secured. If approved by Metropolitan's board at full scale, the program delivery challenge — engineering, construction, permitting, stakeholder engagement, federal grant compliance — will be enormous.
Aging Infrastructure & PCCP Rehabilitation — Metropolitan has issued RFQs for Prestressed Concrete Cylinder Pipe (PCCP) rehabilitation ($70M estimated budget, 2024/2025). Aging distribution system infrastructure, treatment plant equipment, and seismic retrofits (confirmed active CIP projects) represent a sustained, multi-decade capital program need across the service area.
Business Model Transformation — CAMP4W Implementation — CAMP4W is simultaneously Metropolitan's most important strategic initiative and its greatest organizational change challenge. Moving from a traditional integrated water resources planning model to a criteria-based, climate-adaptive capital investment framework requires new analytical capabilities, new stakeholder engagement approaches, and new financial modeling tools.
Member Agency Relations & Rate Pressure — Water rates increased 8.5% effective January 1, 2025 and another 8.5% effective January 1, 2026. Water transactions are at historically low levels (projected 1.34 MAF vs. FY2023/24 actuals near 1.17 MAF — 50-year low). Managing rate increases while water deliveries decline — and rebuilding member agency trust post-SDCWA litigation — is Metropolitan's central financial and political challenge.
Cybersecurity & OT/ICS Risk — Metropolitan operates 5 treatment plants, 20 reservoirs, 24 dams, 830 miles of pipelines, and an extensive SCADA/ICS network across a 5,200-square-mile service area. The 2025 Seismic Resilience Report and ongoing seismic retrofit program (Weymouth Administration Building, $28–35M; Diemer plant seismic work) both signal active infrastructure risk management investment. Water sector OT cybersecurity is a national priority and a direct Deloitte Cyber opportunity.
PFAS & Emerging Contaminants — The 2026 Annual Operating Plan explicitly cites PFAS tracking as a core water quality priority. EPA's PFAS final rule creates compliance deadlines across Metropolitan's 5 treatment plants and multiple source waters. PFAS treatment technology, compliance planning, and stakeholder communication are all near-term advisory needs.
Declining Water Demand vs. Fixed Cost Structure — Metropolitan's water transactions are at or near 50-year lows (1.17 MAF projected for FY2023/24, vs. a historic range of 1.5–2.0 MAF). Fixed costs don't decline with deliveries — this creates sustained rate pressure and financial planning complexity. The business model review under CAMP4W is partially about how Metropolitan finances itself when delivery volumes decline.

Procurement Mechanics & Key Contacts
Procurement Infrastructure

Metropolitan has a formal, structured procurement infrastructure — one of the most accessible among large water utilities in the Western U.S. It uses an e-procurement system providing online access to competitive solicitations and results. Metropolitan procures goods, non-professional services, and professional services through: Request for Bid (RFB), Notice for Bid (NFB), Request for Proposals (RFP), Request for Qualifications (RFQ), and Direct Purchase. Professional services include engineering, architectural, legal, human resources, audit, communications networking, and environmental studies. All construction contracts over $100,000 include a small and/or disabled veteran business enterprise (SBE/DVBE) subcontractor usage requirement. Metropolitan holds quarterly construction workshops (program-level briefings) for its contractor community. Not on PlanetBids — Metropolitan uses its own procurement platform.
